At a time when 15-year-old makeup gurus make us want to step up the ante, and the lockdown has made us lockup our makeup for over 6 months, the meaning of a “no-make up look” is most definitely an AR-flushed look.

Even though we use these filters daily, we don’t really know much about the full-blown profession. So, we decided to dig in. And, of course, we chose to get the scoop from a millennial designer who sold her filter to make-up mogul, Kylie Jenner.

Let’s dive in with make-up artist and Spark AR designer, Sophie Katirai.

On getting a head start into the industry

“I have a Bachelor's degree in Graphic Design and I’ve always been very fascinated with AR filters on Snapchat and Instagram. When I found out that Facebook’s Spark AR platform moved out of its closed beta, allowing designers to publish their own Spark AR effects on the platform, I immediately decided to dedicate at least 4-8 hours a day to learn everything about the platform and start creating.”

(PS- Spark AR Studio is the software used to create Instagram as well as Facebook filters)

You know you’re onto something special when Kylie Jenner endorses your creation

I am a huge Kylie fan; I own almost every shade of the Kylie Cosmetics Lip Kits. She’s also the main reason I started posting makeup looks on Instagram. Back In January, I was up at 6am with my 1-year-old daughter and Kylie had just posted a story about the Stormi Collection with three beautiful moving butterflies in her hair. I found the look extremely beautiful and immediately decided to recreate her look as a filter. I immediately started drawing and animating the butterflies and I also recreated Kylie's makeup, which was done by her makeup artist Ariel Tejada.

I skipped breakfast and lunch and finished the initial design in about 8 hours. Of course, I spent another week perfecting the filter and the movement of the butterfly but I wanted to have it ready on the same day. I posted the filter on my feed, left a comment on Kylie's picture about the filter, and also shared it with Ariel saying his creation inspired the makeup in my filter.

On what it takes to become a Spark AR designer

The simple answer is - the more skills the better. I have a graphic design background and I’m also a painter and a makeup artist so I bring all these skills together when I create my filters. Knowledge of coding and script is also a must.

AR filter creators with a coding background create very technical and complex filters and games. Graphic artists, painters and designers, like myself, combine drawing skills with programming and 3D animation to create art that you can wear on your face.

For my filters, I like to start by drawing all the makeup elements in Illustrator and Photoshop, and that alone requires several hours. Then, I import everything I have created outside of Spark AR and begin the designing and coding process that is needed to complete the project.

On claims that the use of filters causes “Snapchat dysmorphia” since teenagers are getting plastic surgery to replicate their “filter look”

My filters do not include facial warping or deformation. I don’t change the shape or size of the users face or any of their facial features.

In November 2019, Instagram stopped approving filters related to plastic surgery because they seem to have negative implications on mental health. People become dissatisfied with their natural looks because they want to look like the filter-version of themselves with a smaller nose and bigger eyes and lips.

But, the features you see in my filters are your own. I just add layers of makeup and shading, something that is totally achievable in real life with makeup too. You can notice that in the pictures that I post without using my filters.

Pro-tips to achieve the filter look IRL

I love contouring the cheeks with different shades of bronzer and blush, and using eyeliner and strip lashes to achieve dramatic eye looks. I also like to contour the lips with lip liner and different shades of lipstick and top it off with lip-gloss for a more plump look.

“People should be free to decide what they would like to do with their face”

Although I don’t use this type of facial morphing in my filters, I am not against it. I believe people should be free to decide what they would like to do with their face. Besides, filters should be fun. They shouldn’t be taken seriously. I see nothing wrong with having bigger lips by using a filter. You don’t require plastic surgery to get bigger lips and so, I think Instagram should rethink their ban in the future and allow artists and users to make their own decisions.

On culture defining make-up preferences

Women in the gulf region love heavy makeup and have been wearing the full glam face for years before it became so trendy on Instagram. I grew up admiring the beautifully drawn faces and eyes of women around me here. When I used to go home to Canada every summer everyone was more natural and wore a lot less makeup but I see that this is has definitely changed now and women everywhere are experimenting more with makeup.

On diverging from the status quo and sticking to her strengths

I’ve always been the odd artistic one amongst serious, number-focused or technical individuals and I was the only Graphic Designer getting a Master’s Degree in Business Administration in my class at the Sauder School of Business at UBC, Canada. I often felt like my views about the importance of design, branding, color, made people roll their eyes simply because they didn’t understand what I was talking about or thought it was too easy or unimportant. I get this same feeling now too with some of my peers in the Spark AR community. They can write very complex codes but they don’t know anything about makeup and nor are they excited by it. But, luckily I have found a group of amazing artists who are equally passionate about beauty filters and we enjoy exchanging tips and tricks and together our filters get hundreds of billions of impressions per week.
